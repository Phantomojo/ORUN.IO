Big Data ... Big Brother?

Earthman 31 Dec 2016
# Privacy # Society

The advantage of Big Data compared to traditional scientific methods is obvious, however, there are justified concerns that their application will lead to total surveillance and violation of civil liberties. Confidentiality and security of personal information is not guaranteed, not only online, but also in everyday life.

The paradox is that the fundamental principle of Big Data contains a threat: if there is a large amount of data, anonymisation is not possible altogether despite all attempts to make it otherwise.

Scientists are trying to offer solutions to the problem of cyber security, but the reality is that , to exist in the modern world, you have to come to terms with total surveillance. Your mobile phone, a "smart" billboard near your house, a flock of drones with cameras or an ordinary coffee machine – they all are helping "Big Brother" to eavesdrop on your personal life.

Max Isaev With great possibilities came electrical gridlock: it quickly became apparent that using internet for texts and games is one thing, yet with each car transmitting their data in 4K (required for the machine to correctly detect certain objects) from up to 16 videocameras installed on their frames, adding each person with at least one camers on themselves - the traffic reached impossibly large amounts which required proper routing. Quick calculations: 100G optics take more power than 10G optics for the same reach, but it's a matter of 3W against 1W, not so much of a difference, right? Now, switch running 48 x 1Gbps copper ports consumes 300W, but one port of 100G will use 450W and more. One router serve 8 x 10Gbps (optical connection) and consume 600W, but another router with same specifications + 100G port will consume up to 1800W! Low bitrate 4K would require at the very least 25mbps * 16 == 400Mbps per car. There are more than 3 million cars in Shanghai, to open the eyes on each of them would require 1 200 000 000 Mbps (1200000Gbps) == 21.6MegaWatts! But these are only cars, assuming their amount - including carsharing, logistic trucks and other driveless cars - wouldn't increase. What about each person carrying on them at least 1 camera? Shanghai currently has population of 26 millions of people. You know the formula: 26 000 000 x 400Mbps == 10400000Gbps / 100G ports * 1800W == 187MW. "So what?" you might ask "Current nuclear power plants can produce up to 4 Gigawatts!" - well, yes, but you forget that all this data needs to compute. What kind of computer will be able to analyze each bit of data out of these terrific amounts? And, more importantly, how much energy would it consume?
